Reflective Artifact

4.25.2021

Discuss what  you learned and how: 

This semester, I learned multiple things. Many were more minor or served to remind me of things I learned in previous English classes. A lot of the assignments we did were very similar to assignments from English 1301—those worked to reinforce concepts I was already familiar with. Last semester, we planned our research projects in a very similar way to how we did this semester; both research projects started out with a similar brainstorming process which involved re-reading the same content about research and “positionality,” coming up with a line of inquiry, and beginning to find sources for our essays. However, one of the more useful tools that we learned about this semester was the mind-map. In contrast to the brain-dump we did last semester, the structure of the mind-map was very helpful in all of the parts of composing the paper: in the research process, it was helpful to keep on track with the parts of my inquiry that were important; in the writing it helped me to structure the paper and build out a basic outline, and during revision, it helped me to figure out what to keep and remove and/or how to modify what I had in order to improve the flow of the paper. 

I think that one of the more valuable things I learned this semester was from the remix project. I have already written numerous papers and essays, and with that writing comes research. Even though there is always room for improvement, I do generally know how to find sources. A skill that is rather new to me, though, is remixing. As I noted in my last reflection, I don’t think I fully understood the remix project last semester in ENGL 1301, and because of that I didn’t put as much effort into it as I could have. As a result, it wasn’t very engaging to my target audience. This semester, I think that my remix project was of a higher quality, and based on feedback from my peers (who are also my target audience), it is an engaging format. I don’t know anybody my age who would curl up with an academic research paper on the effect of multimedia on languages and just read it unless they had to. However, in some of the feedback I received for my artifact project, the way I delivered my message (in a much more casual format than a paper) actually made them excited about the topic, and they wanted to learn more... I would call that a successful project remix.  

 

Discuss the implications of your learning 

I spent a ton of time on my remix project, but I think it was worth it, because I learned how to take the important information from one format and repurpose it into a new form, one that a demographically different audience would find interesting. The audience for my paper was you, a professor, so it was academic. The audience for my remix project was my peers, so it took on a much more casual form, which worked well for both the context and the content of the information. I think the ability to remix a message from one form to another depending on who I need to communicate with will be an important life skill for later classes I take, as well as for my future jobs.  

 

Discuss how your learning might fit into your future goals and objectives: 

As I just mentioned, I believe the skill of remixing will be valuable in future jobs. I will most likely work as a software engineer, and my dad, who also works in IT has told me that one very important skill he has is the ability to “translate” (or remix) information from the other techies to the customer, who is very rarely as familiar with all of the technical bits. What I learned in this class will hopefully act as a foundation to build upon that skill, which would be valuable to future employers, and even in non-work relationships. 

Reflection 5.3

4.18.2021

What have you learned about your writing/creating/feedbacking process during this experience? 

Last semester I did a pretty poor job on my remix project; I didn’t put as much effort in, and I don’t think I really understood what the goal of the assignment was. Last semester my train of thought was “this is an English class, I am here to write essays,” because that is what English classes are all about in high school. Of course, ENGL 1301-1302 does have a lot of assignments that have to do with research and essay writing, but that isn’t the sole purpose of an English class. What I learned from this semester, and more specifically from this project is how to tailor my message to an audience. Writing academically is a very important skill to have, but only a very few people will sit down with a five-page research paper and just casually read it. The purpose of the remix project was to transform the important information from a paper that many people will have no interest in, into a new form that is targeted at a specific audience. 

This semester, I think I did a much better job on my remix artifact, I understood the purpose of doing the project, and I think that my project communicates the information from my paper to my target audience well, and it was also a good learning experience for me. 

Did you find that your writing process is very similar to your creation process? Is it totally different? Is creating an artifact basically the same thing as writing a paper? Give me the details! 

My writing process is rather different from my creation process. When I am writing a paper, I spend a lot of time figuring out what information is important for my paper. I spend a lot of time researching the topic before I even start writing. When I do start to write, I go many paragraphs at a time with only minimal tweaking before going back to review and revise my writing. In contrast, when I am coding (which is how I made my artifact), I rarely make proper plans ahead of time, and sort of just dive right in. While I am working on a project like my remix project, I am very perfectionist, if possible, making sure that each block of code is right before moving on. Unless I am under a time constraint, I can spend a large amount of time on small sections of code, revising, and sometimes rewriting before I move on to write another section. 

Which texts -- whether they were complete texts or just excerpts -- and videos were especially meaningful for you during this series? What kind of impact did they have on you? 

Of the three videos that we had in series 5, the most helpful video was the micro-lecture in 5.2: “Things to Think About: Giving Feedback on a Multimodal Artifact.” It had a helpful discussion of genre and how it applied to our remix project. Dr. Dibrell broke down clearly what the expectations for a “multi-modal” artifact were. 

How did you use these resources (including peer feedback) to make choices about your artifact? 

Peer feedback, both from the discussion board and friends was useful to keep my project on track. It was quite helpful to have a second, or third set of eyes to tell me if my project still made sense, because it wouldn’t be difficult to make my artifact so that only I would know what was going on. It is easy to know what I mean, and to gloss over finer details that not everybody necessarily knows; for example, in the feedback discussion board, Josephine gave me some feedback that I originally didn't think twice about: she said I should probably give some more background information about who Martin Luther was, because not every person is familiar with him. Growing up in a Christian house, I am already familiar with Martin Luther, but not everybody is. In addition to that, it is easy for people who aren’t familiar with Martin Luther to think I am referring to the equally famous, if not more famous Martin Luther King Jr. In which case, the viewer of my remix project would probably be quite confused. 

Why is it the form/genre that it is? Why that audience? 

The form that I used for my remix project was a fake texting application where pre-scripted messages are sent by various characters to convey the message in an interactive form. The medium I used also acted as a further example of the message I was sending through it. 

Overall, what are you thinking about multimodal artifacts and creating communication that can be aimed at different audiences? What did you find particularly challenging or interesting? 

It is a very useful skill to have. To be able to take the key information from one form, in this case a research paper, and transform it into a completely new form that is made specifically for a new audience is very useful. The main challenge I faced was to decide what genre/form/medium was the best way of getting my message to my target audience. Once I decided to make a fake messaging app, it was a very challenging computer programming project which I had to complete in a limited amount of time. I came across multiple app-breaking and annoying bugs in my project, but with work, those are mostly fixed. My code may be spaghetti code, but it works, and more than likely, you don’t care what the code looks like.

I am somewhat concerned that I won’t be able to find enough research that is mainly focused on reading/writing. There is plenty of research available on how technology affects things like mental health, and sleep schedules, but there research focused on literacy may not be as common.


2) What do you think will be particularly difficult about your research project and why?

I don’t think the researching itself is going to be particularly difficult, because I somewhat enjoy research on topics that interest me, which this one does; however, I do have a problem of going down research “rabbit holes,” and becoming laser focused on a single topic that may only be mildy related to the actual topic of my paper. Going down a rabbit hole is something I need to avoid, and might prove to be a difficulty in for this project.


2) What do you think will be particularly difficult about your research project and why?

I don’t know how clearly I communicated my end goal for the paper. I haven’t done enough research into the topic yet to determine if overall technology has a positive or negative effect on our ability to read and absorb information. Although I remember last semester I didn’t have a completely clear picture of how my research paper would look like when it was complete, but it turned out well; as I continued to research my topic last semester, I had to slightly modify my inquiry to better fit what I learned.


4) Finally, in what ways does your research tie in to the theme of these last two weeks: rhetoric and languaging?

This quote from series 2.2 “Think about how often you use language. Do you text? Do you use any kind of social media? Do you read menus and order lunch? … Language is an incredibly important part of our lives. ” partially inspired my research topic. While I was reading that, I thought “of course,” and the main thing that stood out to me was the parts about technology related communication, which is ever present in our world, especially since Covid 19 pushed schools towards online classes like this one.


 Reading 1: “ Should Writers Use They Own English?”

1) Do you code-mesh when you speak/write? That is, do you switch from one language to another? Or one way of speaking to another? What are the moments/thoughts/cues that trigger this switching? Why do you do it?

Yes, I do. When I am writing a paper for a professor, I use formal language, when I am talking to strangers, I tend to use slightly more formal language than I might if I was talking to a friend or family member, and of course when I am texting, grammar and punctuation, and not-abbreviating are thrown out the window.



Reading 2:“ Bad Ideas About Writing” (Pages 82-87)    

  1. What are your thoughts on the quote "...education, while well intentioned, serves to further the marginalization of certain identities and cultures that do not fit" (83)

    I personally don’t think education on its own is the problem, that is too broad of a statement. I do understand how specifics of education can marginalize some people, for example how Academic English is set as the standard for all English writers. 

  2. How do you think writing instructors might be able to challenge and overthrow white language supremacy in their classrooms?

    I think it is good to expose students to other forms of English that are common among different regions and ethnic groups. It is also a good idea to teach that language isn’t a static entity, it has always changed, and likely always will. Although I understand the authors point of view that argues that Academic English can marginalize students who aren’t native born English speakers, and I would agree that in some cases it does, I think it does make sense to have a relatively standardized system for written communication with other English speakers. Having said that, I don’t think teachers should uphold Academic English as the one and only gold standard of writing in the classroom.
